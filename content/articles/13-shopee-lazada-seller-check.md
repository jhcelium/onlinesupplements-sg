Most supplement purchases in Singapore now start inside a marketplace app rather than on a brand's own website, so learning to check a supplement seller on Shopee and Lazada is a practical skill worth a few minutes. A listing can look polished, carry hundreds of reviews, and still come from a third-party reseller you know nothing about. This guide explains how to read the seller behind the listing, which signals actually matter, and which official tools confirm authenticity. It is general consumer education, not medical advice.

## The platform is not a guarantee

It helps to know how supplements are regulated here. Singapore's Health Sciences Authority (HSA) does not pre-approve or licence individual health supplements before sale; instead, the dealer importing or selling a product is responsible for meeting HSA's safety, quality, and labelling standards. A marketplace simply hosts that dealer — so the listing's home, not the app's logo, is what you are trusting. On both platforms it is worth separating an **official brand store** (Shopee Mall or LazMall storefronts, which brands or authorised distributors operate) from an ordinary third-party seller. The badge is one signal among many, not proof on its own, as we explain in our [seller transparency signals](/seller-transparency-signals).

## Read the seller, not just the listing

Before adding to cart, click through to the store page itself. A few quick checks separate an established retailer from a throwaway account:

- Store age and follower count, plus a consistent rating across many orders.
- A clear business identity — registered name, a Singapore contact, and a stated return policy.
- Product photos that show real packaging with a readable batch number and expiry.
- Honest wording that says a product "supports" or "maintains" health, not that it treats or cures anything.

That last point is a regulatory line, not a style preference: HSA does not allow supplement sellers to claim a product can treat, prevent, or cure disease, and disallows phrases such as "clinically proven." A listing leaning on disease claims is breaking the rules before you have even paid. Our guide to [verifying an online seller](/articles/11-verify-online-seller) walks through these identity checks in more depth.

## Listing red flags at a glance

| Signal on the listing | Why it matters | Reassuring alternative |
|---|---|---|
| "Cures," "treats," "clinically proven" | Prohibited claim wording | "Supports" / "helps maintain" |
| No batch number or expiry in photos | Traceability gap | Clear, readable codes shown |
| Price far below every other seller | Common counterfeit lure | In-line with the market |
| Seller refuses to confirm details by chat | Avoids accountability | Answers promptly and specifically |
| Brand-new store, few real reviews | No track record | Established store, consistent history |

## What HSA enforcement reveals about online listings

The risk on marketplaces is documented, not hypothetical. During Operation Pangea XVII (16 December 2024 to 16 May 2025), an INTERPOL-coordinated action across 90 countries, HSA removed 1,288 illegal health product listings from local e-commerce and social media platforms and issued warnings to 732 sellers. The most-removed categories were skin and hair medicines (37%), antibiotic/antifungal/antiviral creams and tablets (15%), and unregistered contact lenses (13%). The lesson for a shopper is plain: "available and easy to buy" is not the same as "checked and legitimate," and a marketplace badge does not pre-screen every individual listing.

## A pre-purchase verification routine

Use this short routine each time, especially for an unfamiliar brand or seller:

1. Open the store page and confirm it is an official or well-established seller.
2. Search the product or its key ingredient on HSA's illegal-products listing.
3. Check the listing photos for a readable batch number and expiry date.
4. Message the seller to confirm those codes if they are not shown.
5. Compare the price against two or three other sellers for the same product.
6. Keep the order confirmation and packaging photos after delivery.

For a printable version, see our [verification checklist](/verification-checklist), and for what to do with those codes once the parcel arrives, our guide to [batch numbers and recall checks](/articles/12-batch-number-recall-check).

## If a listing or product looks wrong

If a listing carries disease claims, a product arrives without a batch number, or the packaging looks off, slow down. Stop using the product, keep it together with the order record, and search HSA's illegal-products page by product or ingredient. You can report a suspect product or seller to HSA directly through its "Make a report" channel. For any health concern after taking something, speak with a doctor or pharmacist rather than the seller.

## Questions people ask

**Is a Shopee Mall or LazMall store always safe?** These official-store programmes signal a brand or authorised distributor rather than a random reseller, which is reassuring — but treat the badge as one signal alongside batch numbers, honest claims, and Singapore retail presence, not as proof on its own.

**How do I know if a supplement on a marketplace is fake?** There is no single tell. Weigh the seller's identity and track record, readable batch and expiry codes, a market-consistent price, and "supports/maintains" wording, then cross-check the product on HSA's illegal-products listing.

**A listing says "clinically proven" — does that mean it works?** No. HSA disallows "clinically proven" and similar phrasing on supplements, so its presence is a red flag about the seller's compliance, not evidence of effect.
